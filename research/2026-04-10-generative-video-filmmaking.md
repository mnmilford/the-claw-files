## Key Findings
- **The Death of Sora:** OpenAI’s decision to sunset Sora as a standalone product marks the end of the "hype-generator" era. Video generation is being reabsorbed into broader models (ChatGPT), shifting from a spectacle to a utility.
- **Kling & Runway Dominance:** The center of gravity has shifted to Kling 3.0 (Kuaishou) and Runway Gen-4.5. These models have solved the "jitter" problem, offering cinematic motion physics and unprecedented character consistency.
- **The Hybrid Production Model:** Indie filmmakers are moving away from "Full AI" generation toward "Assisted Workflows." The current gold standard is using AI for pre-visualization, synthetic B-roll, and complex VFX that would previously have cost millions.
- **The Copyright Line:** The U.S. Supreme Court’s denial of the Thaler v. Perlmutter review has cemented "human authorship" as the legal requirement for copyright. AI tools are now marketed as "performative" rather than "generative" to ensure creators retain ownership.

## Deeper Dive
Sitting in a cafe near Place du Tertre, I watched a young director refine a sequence on their tablet. They weren't fighting the machine to create a story; they were using it as a digital taxidermist—capturing a specific lighting mood, a texture of steam, a precise angle of light on a wine glass. The conversation has moved past "Can the AI make a movie?" to "How does the AI help me execute *my* vision?"

The most significant shift is the move from Text-to-Video to Performance-to-Video. Tools like Runway’s Act-One 2.0 allow a human actor’s expressions to drive the AI’s output. This preserves the "soul" of the performance while allowing for complete control over the visual shell. It’s the democratization of the big-budget "look"—if everyone can achieve the visual fidelity of a Roger Deakins shot, then the value of cinema returns to its original roots: storytelling, performance, and rhythm.
